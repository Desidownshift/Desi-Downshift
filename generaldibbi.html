Unreserved Realities

Travelling in the general compartment of an Indian train isn't a journey — it's an awakening. It's like a crash course in sociology, psychology, crowd management, and sheer survival. One trip and you come out as a stronger, humbler, slightly squashed version of yourself.

Let's start with the aunties. Ah, the gossip queens of the rails. I once got so emotionally invested in one aunty's monologue about how her son bought her a Prada bag that I almost forgot my stop. She went on about how he sent gifts to all the relatives — masis, buas, and chachis — but also specifically told them not to ask for anything else because he had loans to repay. The emotional drama, the guilt-tripping, the flex — it was better than anything on OTT.

Then there was the transgender person who approached me for money. I showed her my empty wallet with a sheepish smile, expecting her to curse me or at least clap in disapproval. Instead, she surprised me by offering me 20 rupees from her own collection. Yes, I graciously refused, but I was astonished with this act of kindness. That one gesture had more humanity than a hundred panel discussions on empathy.

The standing hours? Torture. You don't stand — you hover. You levitate with one foot angled like a ballerina and the other squeezed between a sleeping uncle's suitcase and someone else's leg. There's barely enough space to breathe, let alone exist. And if you ever find floor space, it's sacred ground.
Speaking of floor space, I once slept on a pile of mineral water bottles stacked like a makeshift yoga mat. My spine has never forgiven me. But at least it wasn't on top of someone's lunch box — small victories.

But here's the thing — the men and women who travel like this every single day to get to work, to keep life moving, to earn just enough to keep the lights on — they have a kind of quiet toughness that you won't find in air-conditioned cubicles. No complaints, no tantrums, just resilience in its most raw and rugged form.

And yes, the ways we travel are often ridiculous — hanging at the door, sitting on top of luggage racks, occasionally becoming one with the train. I not only hang — sometimes I sit on the staircase, which is really scary in a normal compartment but a casual thing in a general compartment. But that's also the charm. It's chaotic, inconvenient, and borderline unsafe, but it's. It's India with the makeup wiped off.

The general compartment doesn't just take you from Point A to Point B. It drops you straight into the soul of India, with all its noise, kindness, stories, sweat, and spirit — unfiltered and unforgettable.
