Taming the Bounce: The Science of F1 Porpoising and the Red Bull Masterclass

What is Porpoising?

Porpoising in Formula 1 is a complex yet fascinating phenomenon, triggered by the powerful aerodynamic forces at work beneath a modern Grand Prix car. While the term might sound quirky, its effects can be brutal—both for the car’s lap times and for the driver's comfort.

At its core, porpoising is the rapid, repetitive bouncing of a Formula 1 car as it speeds down the straight. The nickname comes from the up-and-down motion that resembles a porpoise or dolphin leaping through water. This bouncing isn't caused by the track surface or the suspension alone—it's fundamentally due to the way air behaves beneath the car when ground effect aerodynamics are used.

In modern F1, most of the car’s grip is created by “ground effect.” Here’s the idea: the underside of the car is sculpted into special tunnels called Venturi tunnels. As the car travels quickly, air flows through these tunnels. Imagine pinching a hose—when you squeeze it, the water shoots out faster. The same happens with air in a narrower section under the car: as the car gets lower, the air’s speed increases, which drops the pressure underneath. This creates suction, effectively sucking the car down onto the circuit, generating strong downforce that lets drivers corner at incredible speeds.

However, this system only works within a sweet spot. At very high speeds, the car can get pushed so close to the track that the airflow gets blocked, or “stalls”—think of it like blocking the end of a hose with your thumb. Suddenly, the low pressure vanishes, downforce collapses, and the car pops up on its suspension. Once it rises, airflow returns and the process repeats in rapid cycles. The result is a violent, rhythmic bouncing: porpoising.

The Venturi Principle in F1

The Venturi effect is a foundational fluid dynamics concept: when a fluid (such as air) is forced through a narrow passage, it speeds up and its pressure drops. The underfloor of a current F1 car is shaped with wide channels at the front and rear, but narrowed at the middle—creating a “throat” under the car. As air zooms through this tight section, its speed increases, pressure falls, and a vacuum-like effect glues the car to the track. This kind of tunnel is called a Venturi tunnel. At the back, a diffuser helps control and speed up the exiting air, maximizing the overall suction. The net result is immense downforce, but with a risky sensitivity: a small change in ride height at high speed can suddenly upset the airflow, making or breaking grip.

Why Red Bull Mastered Porpoising

When the 2022 regulations revived ground effect with strict rules on floor shape, many teams struggled to keep porpoising in check. Mercedes, in particular, suffered severe bouncing—drivers described blurred vision and back pain from the rough ride. Their car was designed to run super low to maximize downforce, but this made it get into the “stall” zone too often, triggering major porpoising. Early in the season, they would either run their car low (and bounce violently) or higher (reducing porpoising but losing performance). This dilemma hurt their competitiveness, and their engineers faced a steep learning curve figuring out the right balance.

Red Bull, in contrast, found themselves with a floor concept that was naturally less sensitive to ride height swings. Their approach involved careful floor sculpting: the side edges and tunnels were tuned to maintain stable airflow even as the car got low. Red Bull also cleverly managed the vortices that act like invisible “side skirts,” helping keep the low-pressure zone sealed under the car. As a result, their car could be run at the optimal ride height for maximum downforce with minimal porpoising. The result? A confident, stable chassis that let their drivers push hard, especially on the straights, without the jarring bounce that plagued the competition.

The Result on Track

The difference was obvious at the start of the 2022 season and beyond. Mercedes faced multiple weekends where drivers struggled for comfort, and were forced to compromise with less-than-ideal setups. Red Bull’s drivers, by comparison, enjoyed better stability and could extract more from their package. Over time, teams like Mercedes improved their cars, but the early edge gained by Red Bull in managing porpoising was a crucial part of their championship momentum.

In summary, porpoising in F1 is the byproduct of pursuing massive downforce through the Venturi tunnel principle. Mastery comes from carefully shaping the car’s floor, understanding the airflow, and tuning the suspension to stay within the “safe zone.” Red Bull nailed that sweet spot early, while Mercedes burned valuable races chasing solutions for a problem that, in the new era of wingless downforce, is anything but academic.
